# SECTION 07

# The Architecture Behind the Forecast

The most common misconception executives have about AI inside ERP environments is that the AI itself is the difficult part.

It is not.

The difficult part is the operational architecture surrounding it.

A reliable AI forecasting environment inside JDE EnterpriseOne depends on five things being true simultaneously:

1. The underlying ERP data is structurally trustworthy
2. Ownership of the process is clearly defined
3. Human override pathways are documented
4. Operational workflows are stable enough to model
5. Governance standards exist before automation begins

Without those conditions, AI does not reduce operational risk.

It amplifies it.

# SECTION 09

# Governance Before Automation

The organizations that struggle most with AI adoption are not usually the least technical.

They are the least operationally aligned.

---

## Governance Questions That Must Exist Before Go-Live

### 1. Who owns the model?

Someone must formally own:

* output review,
* exception handling,
* escalation pathways,
* and override authority.

Without ownership, governance becomes performative.

---

### 2. What triggers escalation?

Examples:

* forecast variance above 8%
* delayed draw greater than 72 hours
* committed cost spike exceeding threshold
* abnormal subcontract payment velocity
* declining receivable collection pattern

If escalation triggers are undefined, alerts become noise.

---

### 3. What decisions can AI recommend but never execute?

Recommended only:

* payment prioritization
* liquidity pressure warnings
* cash compression alerts
* subcontract risk clustering
* draw delay probability

Never autonomous:

* lender communication
* payment execution
* banking instructions
* legal approval workflows
* covenant decisions

---

### 4. Who signs off on the final operational model?

This is usually:

* CFO,
* Controller,
* COO,
* or governance leadership.

Not the implementation vendor.
